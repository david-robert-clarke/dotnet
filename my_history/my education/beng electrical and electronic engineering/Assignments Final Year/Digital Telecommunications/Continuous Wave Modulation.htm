For telephony and radio systems a frequency error of <30Hz is usually required.

DSB-AM Modulation

Recovery of the baseband signal from a DSB-SC signal is made more difficult by the absence of the carrier wave at the receiver. One way of overcoming this is to transmit a relatively low power pilot tone carrying the carrier frequency and phase information which can be used to simplify demodulation. The pilot tone must be transmitted outside the frequency band occupied by the modulated signal. If the low frequency response in the signal is unimportant (e.g. in audio) then the carrier can be transmitted within the same spectral band as the DSB-SC signal considerably simplifying detection. This is referred to as double sideband amplitude modulation (DSB-AM).

Amplitude Modulators

Non-linear or Square Law Modulator

The Non-linear or Square Law Modulator generates DSB-AM and has 3 parts (i) A means of adding together the carrier and modulating signals, (ii) A device with a non-linear input/output characteristic and (iii) A bandpass filter to separate out the desired modulation products. A semiconductor diode (as illustrated right) or transistor is commonly used as the non-linear device. The modulating signal and the carrier are added together and applied to the input of the non-linear device.

The applied signal amplitude is made sufficiently small such that it is confined to the non-linear (curved) part of the characteristic for which we can write the output as a polynomial expansion of the input

Commonly only the first 2 terms of this expansion are important (hence square law). Substituting in the the input gives

The bandpass filter at wc removes all the unwanted terms leaving the DSB-AM signal.

When higher order terms contribute some of the "other terms" must be considered. The cubic term for example has distortion products at frequencies wc ± 2wm . If such terms cannot be adequately removed by the filter (generally they cannot) then the additional terms represent distortion caused by overdriving the modulator. Similarly the cubic term also gives rise to intermodulation distortion products between different frequency components of the baseband signal.

In the switching modulator (shown right) the amplifier has a gain of +1 when the switch is closed and -1 when it is open. If the switch is opened and closed at the carrier frequency wc (using a transistor or diode) then the output is the input multiplied by a square wave with amplitude ±1. Ring Modulator

The ring modulator is an example of a double balanced modulator or mixer. A square wave carrier is applied to the centre tapped transformers. During the +ve of the cycle diodes a are switched on and diodes b are switched off and the output equals input signal times +1. During the negative half cycles diodes a are switched off and diodes b are switched on resulting in the output equals -1 times the input signal. Thus we have the product of the carrier and signal giving DSB-SC modulation. The advantage of the ring modulator is that it gives (i)very little distortion, (ii) good stability, (iii) no need for power supplies, (iv) small size and (v) low cost. Since it produces output double sidebands at wc   , 3wc, 5wc    etc and no output at either wc orwm it can be used where wc is just greater than wm.

The ring modulator is very commonly used in DSB-SC modulation since does not require ideal components although their characteristics have to be well matched to suppress the carrier. Its functionality is identical to that of the switching modulator.

DSB-AM Demodulation

On completing this section you should be able to describe how DSB-AM signals are demodulated non-coherently

DSB-AM signals can be demodulated using two different approaches, noncoherent detection and coherent or synchronous detection. The mostly widely used noncoherent detection technique is envelope detection, this is widely employed in commercial AM receivers.

An envelope detector produces an output signal which follows the envelope of the DSB-AM signal which, for a modulation index m<=1 is proportional to the modulating signal. The simplest implementation is based on half wave rectification using a diode followed by an RC smoothing filter as shown here.

The effect of diode is thus to rectify the incoming signal. When the incoming signal is greater than the voltage on the capacitor C the diode conducts and the capacitor charges causing Vc to increase. When the input signal falls below Vc the capacitor discharges and the voltage decreases. The time constant RC is chosen to be much larger than the carrier period but small enough that Vc can follow the highest frequency component of the envelope. With these criteria Vc rises rapidly to the peak of the input signal during the positive half-cycles of the carrier; during negative half-cycles Vc falls slowly as C discharges.

If RC is too large then C cannot discharge sufficiently to permit Vc to follow the envelope (diagonal peak clipping). If too small then some component of the carrier will be passed through

Note that envelope detection requires that the carrier component be large enough to ensure that the whole of the signal envelope sits on the linear portion of the diode I-V characteristic to obtain undistorted detection. Thus in practice m must be less than 1; values up to ~0.95 can be used.

Synchronous Detection

On completing this section you should be able to describe the operation of synchronous detection for DSB-AM signals

The advantage of envelope detection is its extreme simplicity, however, since it can only be used with DSB-AM signals it has the advantage that the modulation efficiency is low. With synchronous detection it is necessary to have the carrier available at the receiver and demodulation is carried out by multiplying this locally generated carrier with the signal. The carrier may be received as a pilot tone or recovered from the signal using square law detection. The key issue is one of maintaining the phase and frequency of the local carrier identical to that used to generate the incoming signal. This is the purpose of the phase-locked loop (PLL) illustrated below. Envelope has been the standard in broadcast receivers since their inception since PLLS are significantly more complicated and, hence, costly to implement. However this situation has changed with modern PLLs readily implemented in chip form.

A phase Locked Loop

The basic PLL consists of a phase comparator and a voltage controlled oscillator in a closed loop control circuit. The phase comparator is a multiplier followed by a low pass filter. The input signal Vi(t) is multiplied by the output signal. Initially their will probably be a phase difference between the two signals so the multiplier output will be

The low pass filter (LPF) blocks the high frequency component and passes the phase component cosf which becomes an error signal ve(t); this changes the frequency of the voltage controlled oscillator (VCO) changing the phase of its output vo(t) towards that of the input vi(t). Noting that frequency is the rate of change of phase, so it is clear that this procedure continues to lock the frequency and phase of vo(t) to that of vi(t).

COSTAS Loop Detector

On completing this section you should be able to describe the operation of the COSTAS Loop detector for DSB-SC

Phase locked loops techniques can also be used to demodulate DSB-SC signals, although the task is complicated by the lack of carrier component in the incoming signal. The Costas loop (shown below) is one PLL implementation for this case.

The incoming signal, is sent to 2 synchronous detectors and is multiplied by two local oscillator signals that are in quadrature with each other. These are provided from one VCO whose output is split in 2, with one component passed through a 90o phase shifter thereby generating the two phase quadrature signals, referred to as the in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) channels.

The output of the in-phase synchronous detector is proportional to Vm(t)cosf and the output of the quadrature detector is proportional to Vm(t)sinf where f is the phase difference between the incoming signal and the VCO.  If the the VCO is initially in-phase with the incoming signal, then the output of the I-channel will  be cosf and the output of the quadrature channel will be 0. If there is a small change in the VCO by and amount f the the output of the I-channel will not change by much (we are at the top of the cosine function) but a small signal will appear at the Q-channel. The phase comparator multiplies together the I-channel and Q-channel to give the resulting signal

The low pass filter in the phase comparator removes all the terms except the sin2f component which is input to the VCO controlling its frequency and phase locking the oscillator output as described for a PLL.

Superheterodyne AM Radio Receiver

On completing this section you should be able to describe the basic building blocks and operation of a superheterodyne AM radio receiver

Broadcast AM radio uses DSB-AM. Individual channels are transmitted on their own specific carrier frequencies and the radio receiver selects the one desired carrier. To achieve this a bandpass filter response is required, however in practice it is very difficult to achieve tuneable RF filters or amplifiers with adequate range and performance. The solution to this is to use the superheterodyne receiver principle, illustrated below.

The basic principle is to use a fixed centre frequency bandpass filter of very high performance such as a ceramic resonator filter (shown right), and to frequency shift the incoming DSB-AM signal to the frequency of that fixed filter - the intermediate frequency (IF). A standard IF of 470kHz is used in the UK for MW AM transmissions (540-1640kHz). Frequency shifting is accomplished by mixing (multiplying) the incoming carrier with the output of a variable frequency oscillator (VFO), the local oscillator (LO).

For a carrier at fc and an IF at fIF the LO frequency fLO must lie either above or below fc by fIF so that multiplication of fc and fLO will shift the carrier and its sidebands to fIF. If fLO<fc then in our example of the MW band the LO would need to tune between 70kHz and 1170kHz. This is an extremely wide band to cover with a ratio between the highest and lowest frequency of 17:1. If on the other hand we choose fLO>fc then the necessary LO tuning range is 1010 to 2110kHz, a ratio of only ~2:1 or 1 octave which is readily achievable. This is the superheterodyne principle.

There is another important precaution that must be taken in implementing a superheterodyne receiver. Our wanted RF signal is at a frequency of  fLO-fIF. If a carrier sits at a frequency of 2fIF above the desired carrier (fLO+fIF) then multiplication of this second carrier with the LO will also produce a signal at fIF and this will also be transmitted through the IF filter. This second carrier frequency is called the image frequency. Interference from this frequency is eliminated by giving the RF input amplifier a bandpass response with a tuneable centre frequency gang tuned with the LO. The response of this filter need not be sharp since it only has to attenuate frequencies displaced from its centre frequency by 2fIF , 940kHz in MW broadcast radio.

The remainder of  the superheterodyne receiver is as before , with an DSB-AM detector (and a synchronous oscillator at fIF if coherent detection is used). Higher performance radio designs may have 2 or 3 stages with decreasing intermediate frequencies to obtain the maximum performance in terms of image frequency rejection and band selectivity. Only one of these stages need be tuned.

SSB-AM Modulation

DSB transmission requires twice the bandwidth of the baseband signal, and since the upper and lower sidebands contain identical information it is clear than the bandwidth could be halved by transmitting only one sideband without loss of information. The resulting modulation is referred to as single sideband amplitude modulation (SSB-AM) and is used in radio, line telephony and telegraphy systems.

The SSB-AM signal is obtained from a DSB-SC or DSB-AM signal by eliminating one sideband and the carrier giving

The most obvious way to generate SSB-AM is to take a DSB-SC signal and filter out one of the two sidebands (shown right), however this is relatively difficult to accomplish, particularly at high frequencies where the two sidebands would be separated by a relatively small proportion of the carrier frequency. The task is made much simpler if the initial modulation is carrier out at  a low carrier frequency, the unwanted sideband filtered out and the remaining sideband remodulated to the required higher frequency as illustrated below. In this example we have an initial signal frequency of 1kHz which is modulated on a carrier of 100kHz to generate the two sidebands at 99kHz and 101kHz. The upper sideband (USB) at 101kHz is filtered our and then used to modulate a carrier at 2MHz generating two new sidebands at 1899kHz and 2101kHz, and again a filter can be used to filter out the upper of these sidebands to generate the USB for transmission. To achieve this in a single step would require separating sidebands at 2099kHz and 2101kHz which is extremely difficult.

Frequency Division Multiplexing

On completing this section you should be able to describe the principles and issues behind frequency division multiplexing (FDM), and how it may be used in analogue telephony.

We have already mentioned that multiplexing is a process whereby a number of independent channels can be combined and sent over the same connumications link. It is clear from the previous discussion that several independent signals can be sent simultaneously over a common transmission path if each one is modulated by a carrier different carrier frequency such that the signal (sidebands) do not overlap. A receiver can then select out one signal from all the others by filtering (e.g. an AM radio receiver). This is the principle behind Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM). FDM enables efficient use of the available;e bandwidth of the communications link; for example a coaxial cable has a bandwidth of several MHz and can accommodate around 1000 voice (telephone) calls of 3.1kHz bandwidth.

If N signals each bandlimited to B Hz are to be transmitted simultaneously we require N carrier frequencies f1, f2,... fN . If DSB-AM is used each modulated signal has a bandwidth of 2B Hz centred at one of the carrier frequencies which must be separated by at least 2B Hz. If SSB is used the signal bandwidth and minimum carrier separation are BHz. Note that the composite signal (multiplex) made up of all the frequency separated signals may itself be frequency shifted (modulated by another carrier fc in which the frequencies f1, f2,... fN are referred to sub-carriers to distinguish them from fc i. At the receiver the incoming signals may be separated by a series of bandpass filters followed by demodulators permitting simultaneous detection of all signals or one signal at a time may be selected by a tuneable bandpass filter. Since practical filters cannot have infinitely sharp cut-off characteristics it is necessary to separate the FDM signals by regions of unused spectrum referred to as guard bands.

Historically FDM has extensively been used for signal multiplexing in analogue telephony although it has now been superseded by time division multiplexed digital signals. The FDM hierarchy, illustrated below, is by international agreement permitting large national and international telephone networks to be interconnected.

A nominal bandwidth is allocated for each voice channel, the audio signal is filtered to be in the range 300Hz to 3.4kHZ leaving a guard bandwidth of 300Hz below and 600Hz above this spectrum to aid channel separation. Each channel is translated to higher frequencies a balanced modulator giving DSB-SC modulation. The Lower sideband (LSB) is filtered out and transmitted. Prescribed carrier frequencies in the 64kHZ to 108kHz range are used giving a multiplex (referred to as a FDM group) of 12 channels in the 60-108kHz frequency range.

This modulation process is repeated to translate each group of channels to higher frequencies using once again a prescribed set of frequencies and retaining only the LSB. For this stage 5 carrier frequencies at 48kHz intervals in the range 420 to 612kHz are used forming a supergroup of 60 independent 4kHz channels. This process may be repeated again to give a 300 channel mastergroup and once more to give a 600 or 900 channel (supermastergroup) FDM assembly.

FM Stereo Radio Transmission

On completing this section you should be able to describe FM stereo radio transmission illustrating the spectral components and a block diagram of an FM stereo encoder and modulator and an FM stereo decoder

Commercial FM Radio uses the internationally agreed 88-108MHz VHF band which is divided to allow one carrier per 200kHz interval. To achieve stereo transmission it is necessary that two separate audio channels (Left L and Right R) must be detectable at the receiver. The approach taken is to transmit L+R and L-R such that a monophonic receiver needs only detect and demodulate the L+R signal while a stereophonic receiver will use the sum and difference of these two to produce separate L and R outputs.

A maximum deviation Df of 75kHz is adopted for commercial FM with an audio base bandwidth of 30Hz-15kHz. The L+R channel occupies the baseband while the L-R signal is carried by DSB-SC on a 38kHz subcarrier. A 19kHz pilot tone is also transmitted to enable synchronous detection of the L-R channel at the receiver. These signals are multiplexed together to produce a composite baseband signal as shown here, and this composite signal is then frequency modulated and transmitted. The FM coder and modulation process is shown here. Note that the audio signal is filtered to emphasise the higher frequencies (pre-emphasis) which improves noise rejection while deemphasis is performed at the receiver reducing hiss. This principle is used in other noise reduction systems such as Dolby.

Carson's rule gives a bandwidth B=2(75+53)=256kHz for the resulting FM signal which is somewhat greater than the 200kHz channel separation. Interference is avoided by the relatively short range of VHF transmission and allocating adjacent carriers to widely separated geographical areas.

As with AM radio, FM receiver systems (shown right) use the superheterodyne principle to achieve frequency tuning with a standard IF of 10.7MHz.  Input signal amplitude fluctuations are compensated using an automatic gain control (AGC) loop in which a signal proportional to the demodulated output level is fed back to a variable gain RF amplifier. This same signal may be used to provide an indication of tuning.

Additionally fluctuations in the local oscillator frequency can be severely detrimental in FM systems so a second feedback loop providing automatic frequency control (AFC) is used to control a voltage controlled oscillator to compensate and stabilise the IF.

FM Demodulation

On completing this section you should be able to describe the principles behind FM demodulation and the various techniques that can be used to achieve it.

Since FM is generated by producing an output frequency proportional to an input voltage, FM demodulation is the inverse of this process by which an output voltage is produced in proportion to the instantaneous frequency of an input signal. Generally the processes involved in FM demodulation may be understood in two equivalent ways: (i) a differentiation step followed by envelope detection or (ii) FM->AM conversion followed by AM demodulation.

Zero-crossing detector

Ideally the FM signal has constant amplitude, however, noise can contribute some amplitude variation which will not be eliminated by the discriminator and will, therefore, contribute to distortion in the demodulated signal. In practical FM systems the FM discriminator is preceded by a limiter circuit to eliminate the amplitude fluctuations. The limiter consists of a comparator circuit which effectively squares of the FM waveform, followed by a bandpass filter.

It is apparent that the FM information in the limiter output is contained at the points where the signal crosses the zero axis, and this forms the basis of a digital FM demodulator called the zero-crossing detector illustrated below.

In the circuit a gating pulse sets a fixed time interval in which the the number of zero crossings in the limiter output is accumulated in a binary counter. The counter outputs is passed to a digital-to-analogue converter (DAC) which produces an output voltage proportional to the number of crossings in the fixed time interval which is, thus, a measure of the instantaneous frequency of the original modulating signal.

Phase-locked Loop FM Demodulator

The third method of FM demodulation is based on the use of a phase-locked loop, which, with the availability of integrated circuit versions, is now commonly used. Recall that the PLL operates by nulling the phase difference between an input signal and the VCO output and that the VCO has a linear frequency-voltage response. In the PLL, shown above, the loop responds such that the signal required to drive the VCO to achieve the phase locking condition is precisely the FM demodulated output.

Sources of Noise

On completing this section you should be able to list and describe a range of external and internal sources of noise occurring in a communications system

Noise may be defined as any unwanted electrical signal which is present simultaneously with the desired signal

Generally, in a communications system the effects of noise are encountered in the receiver where it masks or interferes with the desired signal and so acts against accurate detection and reproduction of the signal.

Noise may be categorised as originating either external or internal to the receiver

External Sources of Noise

These include

Man-made Noise
Electromagnetic emissions from industrial electrical machinery, high voltage transmission lines and switch gear, aircraft and automobile ignitions, fluorescent lighting and numerous electric motors constitute a massive noise source
Atmospheric Noise
Consists or randomly occurring bursts of radio waves originating from lighting discharges and other electrical activity in the atmosphere - commonly experienced inn radio reception where its is referred to as static. Generally occurs as short impulses so that the noise is spread over a wide frequency range.
Solar Noise
The sun radiates electromagnetic energy over a wide frequency range including the spectrum used for communications. In addition the sun has cyclical periods of activity associated with sunspots and solar flares which generate intense bursts of electromagnetic energy.
Cosmic Noise
All stars are intense sources of electromagnetic energy giving rise to noise distributed fairly uniformly across the entire sky. Additionally we receive intense noise from the centre of our own galaxy, from more distance galaxies, and from pulsars and quasars, all of which appear as point sources.

Noise from all of these sources may be picked up by communications antennas, and, in the first two cases particularly, by imperfectly screened electrical circuits.

Internal Sources of Noise

There are many sources of noise internal to electrical circuits

Crosstalk
is caused by electromagnetic coupling between adjacent signal paths. It can be minimised or eliminated by proper design. The coupling is usually either by inductive or capacitive pickup and is prone to happen when metallic conductors are closely spaced and when the signal has significant high frequency content. Digital systems are less prone to this interference than analogues system, and fibre optic systems are immune to crosstalk effects (except where the signals are processed electrically)
Thermal or Johnson Noise
Any resistive electrical component contains electrons which are in random motion due to their thermal kinetic energy. This is zero at absolute zero 0K of temperature. The random motions of the electrons constitute a fluctuating current, the magnitude of which increases with temperature, and therefore any resistive component will have a randomly varying potential difference across it due to this fluctuating current. On the average the potential difference is of course zero and won't register on a dc meter. This random current or voltage is referred to as thermal or Johnson Noise.
The mean square noise current density for thermal noise is given by
The statistical nature of thermal noise means that we must always deal with the mean square or rms. value of the noisy current or voltage since the instantaneous values are unpredictable. Also thermal noise occurs uniformly across the frequency spectrum with a constant power spectral density - referred to as white noise. (actually quantum mechanical analysis reveals that thermal noise has a cut off of ~1012 Hz - well beyond the response of conventional electronics so the white noise approximation is accurate over the frequency ranges of interest to most telecommunications)
Shot or Quantum noise
This the second important fundamental source of noise which occurs in all active electronic devices. This also has, for practical purposes, the properties of white noise. Its origins lie in the discrete nature of the electric charge carriers, the motion of which constitutes a current. There may be slight random variations in their rate of flow across a semiconductor junction giving rise to small random variations in the current.
Shot noise can be modelled as a noise current source with mean square spectral density given by e.g. a sinusoidal current of 10mA (rms.) gives rise to a mean square shot noise current of (2)(1.6x10-19)(1x10-2 )=3.2x10-21A2/Hz. If this current is measured in a bandwidth of 1MHz, the mean square shot noise current is 3.2x10-15A2, or an rms. shot noise current of 5.7x10-8A=57nA. If this flows into a resistance of 1kohm the shot noise power developed is 3.2 pW.
1/f or flicker noise
At low frequencies, typically less than 500hz active devices exhibit a  poorly understood form of noise which has a mean square current spectral density which varies as 1/f (i.e. its power decreases with increasing frequency). It is the result of several different phenomena but it may be thought of as drift noise, arising from the drift, at low frequencies, of the operating points of physical devices.
